# Noirleans: A History

*This document is the source of truth for the world of Noirleans. Names have been changed from historical record. The history is real. The city is not.*

---

## 1. The City (1900–1919)

Noirleans was built by people who should have known better, on ground that made its intentions clear from the start. It floods. It always has. The solution, arrived at over two centuries, was to build anyway and charge the poor to live in the lowest parts.

By 1900, Reconstruction had been over for twenty years and its failure was complete. The Black population of Noirleans lived under a legal architecture designed to ensure that freedom meant nothing in practice — separate schools, separate streetcars, separate everything, enforced by law and by men who didn't need the law. The Creole middle class, mixed-race families who had held property and status since before the Civil War, navigated a narrowing corridor: they were not white, and Louisiana was increasingly uninterested in distinctions.

The city was run by machines. Ward bosses controlled votes, votes controlled contracts, contracts controlled money, money controlled everything. The Old Regular organization dominated city hall the way weather dominates a bayou — pervasively, inevitably, and without much concern for what it destroyed. Judges were bought. Police were purchased. The distinction between city government and organized crime was largely administrative.

The Catholic Church owned a third of the city's property and the conscience of most of its residents. Parishes were political units as much as spiritual ones. The Archbishop had opinions about everything and the leverage to make them matter.

The port was the city's economic heart, and the International Longshoremen's Association was its barely-contained ulcer. Black and white dockworkers had organized together in the 1890s, an arrangement that made the city's power structure deeply uncomfortable. They tolerated it when they had to and dismantled it when they could.

## 2. Prohibition & the Boom (1920–1929)

The Volstead Act made Noirleans rich. The city had never been particularly interested in morality as a governing principle, and Prohibition was not going to change that. Speakeasies operated openly. Police looked away on a rotating schedule that corresponded to payroll. The port — already comfortable moving things that weren't supposed to be moved — adapted effortlessly.

The men who got rich were not subtle about it. Bootleggers bought houses in the Garden District. They sent their children to the right schools. They donated to the Diocese. Some of them were invited to join the right clubs after a decent interval; others weren't, and held the grudge. The distinction between old money and new money in Noirleans was mostly about how many generations back the original crime occurred.

The mob — which in Noirleans meant primarily the Sicilian families who had arrived in the 1890s and survived the 1891 lynchings to build something formidable — institutionalized. They weren't street criminals anymore. They were businessmen who happened to employ violence as a human resources strategy. They had accountants. Some of them had lawyers who had gone to Tulane.

Jazz played through all of it. The music came from the Black community, traveled through the brothels and gambling houses of the old red-light district before its closure, and spread everywhere. White bandleaders got rich recording it. The men who invented it mostly didn't. That was also not going to change.

The Crash came in October 1929. Noirleans had been rich. Then it wasn't.

## 3. The Crash & the Lean Years (1929–1933)

The Depression did not arrive in Noirleans the way it arrived in the rest of the country, because Noirleans had never been as far from the bottom to begin with. The Black community had been in a depression since Reconstruction. The dockworkers had been in a depression since the union drives of the 1910s. What changed was that the middle class joined them.

Bread lines formed. Men who had worn good suits to good jobs stood in them, which was humiliating, and the humiliation mattered to them more than was useful. The speakeasies stayed open — they'd survived worse — but the bootleggers who owned them were watching their margins. Prohibition would end in 1933. They needed to plan.

Labor organized. The longshoremen struck in March 1932, shutting down the port for four months. They were broken by a combination of Pinkerton men, parish deputies, and a judge who had been on the machine's payroll since 1921. Three men were killed on the picket line. The union was not certified. The men went back to work at lower wages than before the strike, because that was the point.

The federal government arrived with the New Deal, which meant money and strings and outsiders asking questions. Some of the money was real — roads, bridges, public buildings. Some of it evaporated in transit, which was expected. The outsiders with questions were not welcome. Noirleans had its own arrangements and did not need Washington explaining things.

Prohibition ended in December 1933. The bootleggers had already pivoted. The moralists despaired. The speakeasies got licenses. Nothing actually changed.

## 4. The Short Years (1930–1935)

Howie Short came from the north parishes, a country lawyer who had figured out something the Regulars and the old guard hadn't: poor people vote too, if you give them a reason.

Short was a demagogue, but he was a demagogue who built things. Roads in parishes that had never had paved roads. Schools in parishes where children had been walking miles on dirt tracks to reach a one-room building. A charity hospital that actually treated the poor. He taxed the oil companies and the utilities and used the money for this, which made him enemies that lasted.

His machine was total. Every state job, every contract, every appointment ran through Short's office. Judges who weren't with him found their dockets suddenly complicated. Legislators who voted against him found their districts' road projects delayed. He was not subtle and he didn't need to be. He had the votes and he had the levers and he used both.

Who loved him: the rural poor, who had roads now. Certain labor organizers, who had a governor who at least pretended to care whether working men ate. Black Noirleaners, cautiously — Short wasn't an integrationist, but he was somewhat less interested than the average Louisiana politician in making their lives actively worse, which in context was notable.

Who despised him: old money, which had never been taxed like this. The Diocese, which found him impossible to control. The Regular machine in Noirleans, which he had humiliated and tried to dismantle. The federal government, which found his populism alarming and his tax schemes suspicious. Standard Oil, which found him expensive.

He was shot in the State Capitol building on a Sunday evening in September 1935. The man who shot him was Dr. Emil Voss, a young physician from a respectable family. Voss was killed on the spot by Short's bodyguards — thirty bullets, according to the papers, though no one counted carefully. Short died two days later. He was 42.

Nobody knows why Voss did it. His family says he didn't. Short's men say Voss was an assassin sent by the old guard. The old guard says Short's own security fired the fatal shot by accident and blamed Voss to cover it. The truth is probably simpler and uglier and nobody wants it.

What is certain: the Voss family is now paying for it regardless. They've lost Emil's medical practice. His brother lost his job at the courthouse. His widow has received letters. Their house in Metairie was vandalized twice in October. Short's loyalists need someone to punish and the courts aren't moving fast enough.

## 5. The Fault Lines (1935 — Present)

The city as it stands, six weeks after the assassination:

**Race.** Jim Crow is the law and the custom. The Great Migration is pulling people north — Detroit, Chicago, New York — and the pace has picked up since the Depression. Those who stay navigate a system designed to exhaust them. The Creole community maintains its own institutions, its own churches, its own social register, and watches both the white and Black communities with the wariness of people who have learned that alliances are temporary.

**Machine politics.** Short's organization still controls most of the state apparatus, but the top is gone and there's no clear successor. Lieutenants are positioning. Rivals who spent years waiting are making moves. The Regulars, who Short spent years trying to destroy, are seeing an opportunity.

**Labor vs. capital.** The longshoremen haven't forgotten 1932. New organizing is happening, quieter than before. The port bosses are watching it and responding with familiar tools. The CIO is trying to get a foothold. Everyone is waiting to see how hard the company men will push back.

**Old money vs. new money.** The bootlegger fortunes are three years into respectability. Some of them have been absorbed — married into the right families, donated the right amounts. Others are still outside looking in, which makes them unpredictable. The Depression pruned some old fortunes badly. The embarrassments are not discussed.

**Church vs. vice.** The Diocese disapproves of most things and has the moral authority to say so publicly. The city's appetite for gambling, drinking, and the attendant trades has not diminished. The tension is stable and mutually profitable — the Church collects, the operators operate, and the police manage the equilibrium.

**Federal intrusion.** New Deal agencies have offices in Noirleans now. The FBI has been expanding. Neither institution is loved. Both are watched. Men who cooperate with federal investigators have short careers in local politics.

**Assassination backlash.** Short's loyalists are running on grief and rage, which is a dangerous combination. The Voss family is the nearest available target. Beyond them, anyone perceived as having been neutral or relieved by Short's death is being noted. This is not a city where it is comfortable to have been quietly glad about something.
